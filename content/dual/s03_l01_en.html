Two categories of deafblindness

The definitions say nothing about how deafblindness is acquired. It is common to distinguish between congenital and acquired visual and hearing impairments.

  • The term congenital deafblindness is used when people are born deafblind or when their combined hearing and vision impairment occurs before spoken, signed or other visual forms of language and communication have developed.
  • The term acquired deafblindness is used when people are born deaf or hard of hearing and later in life experience deteriorating sight.

Causes of deafblindness

The most common eye diseases in deaf-blind children in East Africa are:

  • Cataract.
    The lenses are unclear. Cataract can lead to glaucoma.
  • Glaucoma.
    High pressure in the eye. If not treated, can lead to blindness.
  • Coloboma.
    Cleft in the eye that leads to decreased visual field and visual acuity.
  • Optical nerve atrophy.
    That leads to decreased visual field and visual acuity.
  • Retina detachment.
    Leads to total blindness. It is often a consequence of other eye diseases.
  • Cerebral visual impairment.
    The visual impairment is not in the eye itself but in the nerves leading to to the visual part in the brain, or in the visual brain itself. this condition leads to problems with understanding visual information, problems with sustained visual attention and with sensory integration.
  • Rubella.
    Causes may be related to mother’s pregnancy such as congenital and retinopathy of prematurity.

    Other causes of deafblindness are:

  • Rare diseases and infectious diseases that involves both sight and hearing impairments.
  • Random combinations of various injuries / diseases that affect both visual and auditory function.
  • Usher syndrome. People with this syndrome experience a gradual loss of their hearing or they are born hard of hearing or deaf, and sight is gradually reduced as a result of eye disease retinitis pigmentosa.
    Usher syndrome has three clinical subtypes:
    • Usher syndrome I. People are born profoundly deaf, and begin to lose their vision in the first decade of life. They also exhibit balance difficulties, and learn to walk slowly as children, due to problems in their vestibular system.
    • Usher syndrome II. People are not born deaf, but do have hearing loss. They do not seem to have noticeable problems with balance; they also begin to lose their vision later and may preserve some vision even into middle age.
    • Usher syndrome III. People with are not born deaf, but experience a gradual loss of their hearing and vision; they may or may not have balance difficulties.
  • Age-related diseases affecting vision and hearing.

Limitations caused of deafblindness

Deafblindness entails major limitations and may affect all or some of the following:

  • Communication
  • Access to information, education and employment
  • Orientation and mobility
  • Learning
  • Social relationships
  • Isolation
  • Confidence and self-esteem
  • Independence in daily living skills
  • Access interpreters and to aids and equipment because of cost

Deafblindness is concentration demanding and exhausting. The limitations can all cause fatigue and frustration in dealing with everyday situations.

Use of other senses

In addition to vision and hearing smell, taste and touch are the senses which is most mentioned.

Aristotle (384 BC - 322 BC) is credited with the traditional classification of the five sense organs: sight, smell, taste, touch, and hearing.

Humans also have awareness of balance, pressure, temperature, pain, motion and awareness of the position and movement of the parts of the body (the sense of kinesthesia).


The tactile sense

The sense of touch or the tactile sense is of special importance for people with visual and hearing impairment. Touch is distributed throughout the body and nerve endings in the skin and other parts of the body which transmit sensations to the brain. Some parts of the body are more sensitive than others. The fingertips are among the organs with the greatest concentration of nerve endings and therefore of great importance of the deafblind.
