What is communication?

Communication means to share information; emotions, expressions, ideas and memories. It happens between two or more partners where the partners are active in the process and together they co-create or develop communication.

Humans are social beings and communication with others is an essential and important aspect of our lives.

The ability to communicate is essential to convey our opinions, needs and feelings. Through communication we create social relations and make us participate in the life that surround us.
Communication difficulties appear primarily when the hearing is impaired. If the sight is reduced too, the ability to communicate residues further.

What is communication used for?

Communication is used for basic, social interplay to ensure that the person accesses natural contact, shared emotional experiences and attachment. Communication can be useful for persons with deafblindnesss in the following ways:

  • obtaining personal needs.
  • informing about what has already happened, what is happening and what is going to happen.
  • commenting on the interest of the person with deafblindness.
  • sharing experiences in a conversation.
    To communicate about what you experience together is important for developing self-esteem and for developing communication further.

Why communication is the main focus in services for persons with deafblindess

Communication is the main goal because it includes emotional, social and cognitive development. Communication is important because it:

  • creates bonding with parents, family members, houseparents and teachers.
  • promotes social interaction. The deafblind child learns to establish and sustain contact and social rules like turn-taking.
  • support the development of self-esteem. The child feels that she can master things on her own, she is understood and appreciated by her partner and she can influence his/her own life.
  • builds relationships and awareness of other persons. It is the precondition for developing empathy.
  • promotes understanding of the physical and social surroundings, building up mental images and concepts of what they experience.

Main methods of communication

Persons with deafblindess have many different ways of communication. The methods they use vary, depending on the causes and function of their combined vision and hearing loss, their backgrounds, and their education.

The main methods of communication with a person with deafblindness or between two deafblind people are:

  • Tactile Sign Language
    The person with deafblindness puts his or her hands over the signer’s hands to feel the shape, movement and location of the signs. See an example of tactile sign language in this Spanish-speaking video.
  • Sign language (based on visual communication) This is a visual mode of communication expressed by the use of hands, facial expression and body movements. It has its own rules of grammar, structure and the way of expression.
  • Tactile Fingerspelling
    The person with deafblindness may prefer to put his or her hand over the fingerspelling hand, or on the signer’s palm, or cup his or her hand around the signer’s hand. Usually people who are deaflbind prefer tactile fingerspelling because sometimes sign language can be difficult to learn.
  • Screen Braille Communicator (digital)

    The Screen Braille Communicator is a communication device designed for use by individuals who are deaf blind to communicate with the sighted persons

  • Writers and tactile interpreters

    These are people who interpret tactile communication and oral communication to facilitate communication between those who are blind and deaf or hard of hearing and other people. The tactile interpreter puts a hand either on top of or below the other person's hand in order to convey and receive information from the person who is deaf blind.

  • Note takers

    Note takers are devices that are used by persons who are deafblind to take notes. These can also be people who assit to take notes for persons who find difficulties in writing. See pictures Google

  • Haptic communication
    The idea behind the haptic communication is to share visual and auditory information from the environment, using the haptic signals in parallel with the ordinary communication. It is meant to serve as a complement to the spoken or signed language in a communication situation.
    Haptic communication does not replace speech or sign language. It is about creating meaning and context, to get a better picture of the situation and the room you are in.
    It will strengthen and complement other kinds of communication between human beings.
    Haptic communication takes place through the body contact at neutral zones in the body like back, shoulder, hand, knee or foot. It is bounded to the situation, context, place and orientation.

Haptic communication can be used in:

  • providing feedback and communication
  • describing colours
  • characterizing and feeling
  • mobility
  • Describe food and drink
  • describing a room
  • mapping the surroundings
  • telling what´s happening
  • telling the weather

The five haptic articulation areas


  1. Shoulder, upper arm – is reserved for linguistic signals, to indicate names, items etc.
  2. The upper back, or the whole back - is used to draw maps, such as over a room or surroundings. The upper back, between the shoulders, is used to indicate direction, right/left and to instruct the child on what to do e.g walk
  3. The hand as proform for face – can be used to signal facial expressions, or to create maps, localisation of items in the surroundings.
  4. At the very front of the knee, the outer part of the knee. In a sit-down position: feedback/response, smile/grumpy, mouth/laughter, spellings, initials and more.
  5. The instep; foot to foot, response to idicate “yes/no”

  • Tadoma
    This is a form of communication that persons with deafblindess who have little or no usable vision, use to speechread another person by touch. They put their thumb on the other person’s chin, and their fingers on the other person’s cheek to feel the vibrations of the person’s voice and the movement of their lips.

    Identification of ways of communication among new learners

    The first step in communicative development is to establish and maintain the contact. The first communicative expressions of the child will be natural expressions which has to be discovered and reacted upon by the partner. The partner reacts on all the ways the child can express himself and tries to introduce more and more advanced communication systems leading to sign language for the children that have good cognitive capacities.

    Source: FSDB/SHIA Publication, 1998

    Each individual is different

    To maintain the contact with others, sound residues needs to be recovered as much as possible. Even if both hearing and vision are lost, the communication with others can be maintained. Be aware of each individual is different and requires individual adaptations and communication methods.

    Tips for communicating with people who are deafblind:

    • Make sure you have the person’s attention before trying to communicate with her/him.
    • Gently touch the top of the person’s arm and identify yourself clearly.
    • Keep in physical contact during the conversation.
    • Speak slowly and clearly in short sentences, but don't shout.
    • Be clear about the theme you're talking about and refer to known references.
    • Use gestures and facial expressions to support what you are saying.
    • Speak one at a time.
    • Avoid background noise.
    • Use the time needed for the conversation.
    • Always reply back.
    • Repeat phrases or re-phrase the sentence if necessary.
    • Talk face to face.
    • Keep your face visible and be sure it is lightened.
