Visual impairment refers to a significant functional loss of vision. This functional loss varies from low visual loss to total vision loss.

  • A person with low visual functional loss has a possibility to see based on refractive corrections.
  • Blindness refers to inability to see at all or to merely perceive light.

Causes of visual impairment

Visual impairment can be caused by various factors including prenatal, neonatal and postnatal factors.

  • Prenatal causes may be consequences of the living style of pregnant mothers which affect the child before birth.
  • Neonatal causes may occur during birth especially issues related to infections and accidents or trauma.
  • Postnatal causes may be environmental factors a person encounters in the surroundings including nutrition, accidents, infections or diseases.

Some of the causes of visual impairment are as follows:

  • Cataracts are congenital and pediatric pathology that describes the greying or opacity of the crystalline lens In case of adults, cataracts are associated with aging. As we age we are likely to experience cataract. However, there is a possibility of reducing risk to experience cataract by avoiding smoking and extensive exposure to sunlight.
  • Retinitis pigmentosa is a group of genetic disorders that affect the retina's ability to respond to light. This inherited disease causes a slow loss of vision. Retinitis pigmentosa causes permanent changes to your vision, but how much and how quickly can really vary from person to person. These retinal changes can affect your side vision, which makes it harder for you to see in dim light or the dark, and central vision, which causes difficulty with detailed activities such as reading or watching television.
  • Congenital rubella syndrome (CRS) can occur in a developing fetus of a pregnant woman who has contracted rubella. CRS can cause sensorineural deafness (58% of patients) or eye abnormalities — especially retinopathy, cataract, and microphthalmia (43% of patients).
  • Usher syndrome is a relatively rare genetic disorder caused by a mutation in any one of 10 genes resulting in a combination of hearing loss and visual impairment, and is a leading cause of deafblindness. Usher syndrome is incurable at present. Usher syndrome is characterized by hearing loss and a gradual visual impairment. The hearing loss is caused by a defective inner ear, whereas the vision loss results from retinitis pigmentosa (RP).
  • HIV/AIDS may cause both visual and hearing impairment. AIDS refers to a set conditions that weaken the immune system of an individual who has been infected with an Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV). It is a life threatening condition. In East Africa region the governments have mounted free services for HIV/AIDS related conditions. In places where services are provided, people rarely face difficulties. Services provided include counselling supplements, and medication.
    Poverty may affect the general care of people with HIV/AIDS. Deterioration of body immunity may occur, and may have impact on vision. In Kenya for example, there are disability friendly centres for HIV/AIDS and related conditions for persons with disability.
  • Malaria can cause brain hemorrhage, although rarely. Brain hemorrhage is sometimes associated with severe cerebral malaria (caused by infection with Plasmodium falciparum, the most deadly form of malaria). This bleeding in the brain could cause blindness—whether the visual impairment is permanent or not depends on the extent of the bleeding and how much damage it caused.
  • Corneal opacification can cause anything from minor irritation to vision problems and even blindness. Corneal opacification is also referred to as scarring or clouding of the cornea. The cornea is the clear, dome-shaped area that covers the front of the eye. Light passes through the cornea before reaching the retina in the back of the eye, and so it must remain clear so light can pass through. Injury, infection, and certain eye diseases can cause corneal opacities.
  • Refractory errors include myopia (nearsightedness), hyperopia (farsightedness) and astigmatism (blurred or distorted vision). Myopia also called ‘nearsightedness’ it is most common refractive error of the eyes. It is a condition in which objects which are close are seen clearly, but objects which are farther away seem to be blurred. This condition can be corrected by eyeglasses, or contact glasses. There has been speculation whether myopia is inherited. Reports show that myopia is not inherited; it is a condition which may be due to excessive reading and doing other close to eyes work. It ranges from mild, moderate to severe conditions.

    Hyperopia is also called farsightedness, a common type of refractive error where distant objects may be seen more clearly that objects which are near. Therefore people with hyperopia can see distant objects very well, but experience problems with objects that are close. Hyperopia is considered to be a common vision problem. It can be corrected by eyeglasses and contact lenses, but it is not possible to cure the situation. It is also reported that surgery may reduce dependency to glasses.


    Astigmatism is a common and usually minor condition of the eye that cause blurred or distorted vision. It is a common type of refractive error. The condition in which they eye does not focus light evenly onto the retina. In case of astigmatism, the following may be remedies to the situation: use of corrected eyeglasses, contact lenses and refractive surgery. The signs and symptoms of a person with astigmatism include headache, eyestrain and blurred vision at all distances.
  • Injuries can cause visual impairment. People who have been involved in accidents often need rehabilitation, training in communication and activities of daily living.
  • Infections are conditions which may contribute to visual impairment. For example, for children, causes may be related to their mother’s pregnancy such as congenital rubella syndrome and retinopathy of prematurity. Other infections include leprosy and trachoma. It is approximated that one million people with leprosy live in developing countries including Kenya, Tanzania and Uganda. In case of trachoma the eyes are infected by bacterium chlamydia trachomatis. Trachoma infection is through contact with an infected persons’ eye and nasal discharges and also by flies in the tropics. It is possible to avoid risk of trachoma infection through public health services and observing.
  • Age-related macular degeneration is the leading cause of severe vision loss in people over age 60.
    A form of macular degeneration is characterized by the presence of yellow deposits, called drusen, in the macula. A few drusen may not cause changes in vision. However, as they grow in size and increase in number, they may lead to a dimming or distortion of vision that people find most noticeable when they read.

  • Glaucoma is a congenital and pediatric eye disease characterized with increased pressure within. Glaucoma associated with intraocular pressure. For children, most common cause of pediatric glaucoma is cataract removed surgery.
  • Diabetic retinopathy is an ocular manifestation of diabetes which affects up to 80 percent of all patients who have had diabetes for 10 years or more. It can eventually lead to blindness. Diabetic retinopathy often has no early warning signs. Even macular edema, which can cause rapid vision loss, may not have any warning signs for some time. In general, however, a person with macular edema is likely to have blurred vision, making it hard to do things like read or drive. In some cases, the vision will get better or worse during the day.

Other causes for visual impairment are:

  • Rubella syndrome (also German measles). Causes may be related to mother’s pregnancy such as when infected with the rubella virus during early pregnancy.
  • Leprosy. Approximately one million people live with leprosy in developing countries including Kenya, Tanzania and Uganda. Leprosy is a highly infectious disease caused by bacteria known as Mycobacterium leprae and Mycobacterium lepromatosis. Without early medical intervention, it will lead to permanent to the eyes, skin, nerves and limbs.

  • Albinism in humans is a congenital disorder characterized by the complete or partial absence of pigment in the skin, hair and eyes. The lack of necessary pigments in the eyes has impact on the sight.

    Albinism is about the body not having enough melanin (pigment) which is a chemical substance in the body. This condition is inherited and there is no treatment for the condition. The father and mother have has genes that lead to albinism. So the fusion of the two sets of genes is what usually causes a child to be born with albinism. Albinism is not specific to human beings. It happens with animals, birds, plants and every living organism. The colour of the skin does not reflect the nationality or origin of a person with albinism. For human beings, the lack of the necessary pigment can increase the risk for skin cancer.
    However, it also has an impact on the sight.

    It leads to eyes not being stable and having a frequent movement. This condition is known as nystagmus. It has a consequence of not being able to see properly. There is noe alignment, that is why you can see that a person who wants to look to an object may turn the head sideways, and maybe just one of the two eyes are stronger than the other so they have to look with that one eye. That is why it is very difficult for them to be focused when they look at an object.
    When a child is born with albinism, the eyes are much stronger, but with age this power size decreases and it becomes weaker when exposed to the sun. People with albinism have to change their spectacles more often than people without. The power which remains may finally disappear completely so in the end they will not be able to see.
    Persons with albinism should be protected from the sun by wearing hats and sun glasses to avoid the adverse effects of the sun’s rays on their skins and eyes respectively. Application of the sun-screen lotions on the skin is recommended for skin protection. Furthermore, dressing should cover most of the body parts as well to protect them from the sun’s effects, on the skin.
    In the recent past, the lives of persons with albinism in East Africa have been at risk due to myths and beliefs associated with witchcraft. Consequently, there is a need for sensitization of communities about albinism as a condition which does not make a person different from other persons except for lack of skin pigmentation.
  • Poisoning is also mentioned among possible causes of visual loss. Methanol is one of the chemicals, which if consumed, may lead to blindness.
  • Vitamin ‘A’ deficiency. Xerophthalmia is the eye condition which results from Vitamin ‘A’ deficiency. It manifests in the scarring of the cornea as a result of the dryness of the eye.

Simulate common eye diseases with your smartphone

If you have a smartphone, you can simulate common eye diseases as cataract, glaucoma and retinitis pigmentosa. The Norwegian Union for the Blind has developed an app, which shows how six different diseases can be experienced. Choose one of the diseases in the app and direct the camera towards an object close to you. The view can be adjusted from unaffected to severely affected. The app will give you a unique experience about how it can be visually impaired.
