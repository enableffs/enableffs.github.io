Support from family and friends

Being blind can be challenging in different social settings. Blindness does not just make it difficult to participate in educational and work related tasks, but also in activities outside school or work settings. This may lead to low self-esteem.

Peoples attitude to blindness can be challenging. Lack of knowledge regarding blindness may lead to misconceptions and negative attitudes, like thinking this is a contagious disease. Attitudes like this from colleagues, friends and family makes it even more difficult being blind.

Emotional support and positive attitudes from friends and family are very important for the blind, and can play a key role in helping to adjust and handle the situation.

Moses, a blind student at Kyambogo University, sits on a chair and tells his story.

The social setting was not easy at first. It wasn't easy. Because...you know people are not yet sensitised They would think…ehm..when you associate with a blind person maybe you will also get it. They thought it was transferable like these airborne diseases. They would even fear touching on your white cane. They could say, don't touch it. You might contract the problem. So, at first it wasn't easy. They had negative attitudes towards blind students at that time. And that was a challenge. Actually, we were discriminated at first.

But with time we reported the matter to our head teacher. She called upon all the school boys and girls. And she sat us down. And she tried to sensitise our fellow colleagues. That socialising with us would be better. And she showed them that disability is not inability by making us do certain things that they could not do like playing instruments. Me, I would play drums and everyone near me would feel like dancing because I was a hero in that matter. And they could not. The other colleagues could not do that, yet they could see. And our head teacher and the other teachers advised the pupils to not discriminate us. Then they started slowly slowly coping up. And now up to date socialisation in that school between persons with visual impairment and other ordinary students is at least at a better stake.

Disability is not inability.

Family and friends have an important role in showing that being visually impaired does not mean inability. They should be positive to persons with visual impairment and give them the opportunity to work and experience the world.

It is possible to help them through daily activities such as survival skills like feeding, mobility, and self-care. Other skills include social life skills like sharing and interaction.

Overprotection

John M. Muga is a teacher at The Department of Visual Impairments, Kenya Institute of Special Education (KISE). He has discovered there is a lot of overprotection on the part of those who lose sight.
Listen to him or read the transcribed interview.

Through my recruitment efforts, which are moving away from KISE to those individuals that been referred to us, I have come to discover there is a lot of overprotection on the part of they who lose sight. Either parents enclose them to a limited area in an effort to prevent them for hurting themselves maybe they are able to move around. Or even reducing the kind of task they should exposing them to on a daily basis. This one is even more disabling, everybody should understand this is even more disabling. Because some of the clients we are having, you noticed somebody have added a lot of weight, (don´t) mobilising themselves. Another one does not want to interact with people widely. They appear to be closed in a box. But we are bringing every effort to expose them to facial activities, exercises and also expose to, encourage them to interact with other people so that they can really discover themselves and receive their own selves. We need support from those organisations that understand that we can be able to intervene positively in this area.
