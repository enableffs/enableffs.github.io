It is likely to identify a person and learners with visual impairment through the following aspects:

  • Complaints
  • Appearance of the eye(s)
  • Physical behaviours
  • Screening

Complaints

The person with visual impairment may have repeated complaints like eye scratching, burning, and itchy skin. Further complaints can be about seeing blurred images or objects being in double images.

Appearance of the eye(s)

The appearance of the eye may also indicate something is wrong with the eye. For example, The eyes maybe watery or seemingly inflamed, swollen or even appearing crossed. It is important to consult the eye practitioners for examinations. Eyelids do not completely cover the eyes when closed.

Physical behaviours

It is likely possible for a person with visual impairment to exhibit the following behaviours, among others:

  • Tend to feel objects with their hands instead of looking at them.
  • Rubbing eyes excessively.
  • Turning or tilting the head when using eyes to see or focus on an object.
  • Tend to have difficulty with activities which demand use of eyes.
  • May show difficulty in reading (to one who knows to read).
  • When reading, tends to hold books too close to eyes.
  • When watching television, may tend to be very close to the television.
  • May show excessive blinking.
  • When looking at things, may hold them in a particular position.
  • In the classroom, may be constantly asking mates about what is going on around.
  • Tend to hold objects very close or very far to see them.
  • Tend to bump into objects and having trouble seeing at night.

Screening

The most important way of getting to know whether a person has a vision problem is through screening.

This refers to a method of identifying children with visual impairment or eye conditions that are likely to lead to visual impairment. The aim of screening is to identify the visual problem. Both children and adults can undergo vision screening.

Through screening, the following may be categories of problems to be identified:

  • neonatal conditions
  • risk factors
  • refractive errors
  • colour vision abnormalities

Early intervention

Early intervention can mean both interventions in early years of human life or intervention in early stages of visual loss. If a parent, caretaker or teacher notices any anomalies or difficulties in a childâ€™s vision, he/she should consult an eye doctor or vision specialist for assistance.

Early intervention is extremely important. The reason is: if it is in children then it is likely possible to prepare interventions which may help the child.

If the child is at school, special programs will be effective for child development in all domains: intellectual, physical, social and emotional. Teachers can refer the child with a visual problem to Resource Centres (RCs). In the case of Kenya in East Africa, the children can be referred to Education Assessment Resource Centres (EARCs) for vision assessment.

In case the early intervention is to an adult who has just started developing signs of visual impairment, there is a possibility to handle the situation.
