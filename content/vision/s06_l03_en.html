Operating computers without using vision


Frida Gatwiri Kiambati is Adaptive Technology Instructor at Visual Impairment Department at KISE. She introduces computers to learners who have become blind late in life. They learn to operate the computer without using vision.

Listen to the interview with Frida G. Kiambati

The first thing I introduce them to and which is very much individualized is the keyboard, because that gets us to everything. Everything that a person with visual impairment has to do it has to be done through the keyboard. Maybe I didn´t mention that they don´t use the monitor, they also don´t use the mouse, simply because the monitor need some visuals so that a learner would be able to check what is displayed. At same way the mouse require some vision too, so a learner is able to follow the cursors movements on the screen. But then my learners are not able to see, so they don´t have that vision. That is why we even put the monitor off and we don´t use the mouse.
But with the keyboard we can´t introduce them too fast, so once they have monitored all the keys on the keyboard, they will in a weeks time or two, it takes a lot of time there, because that is the very key.
Once they are through with that now we introduce them to the other parts of the computer and then we get to know more about introduction to computers.
Then after that I take them the third package, which is introduction to adaptive technology so that they are able to understand and appreciate that. They don´t have the vision and they remain to rely on their hearing so that they are able to operate the computer normally, just like any other sighted person.
Then after that the other packages follow like an introduction to Word, because now after they have known how to use the keyboard properly, they are been also introduced to the concept of information and communication technology and starting up the machine including including the wiring, I get them to touch where the wires are. I get them to understand the connection so that the computer is powered on. Right from the socket where the xxx (?) is to the purpose of all that and all the connections.

Screen readers on PCs and laptops.

Through advancement of technology, computers with certain software are now doing different activities that can be useful to people with visual impairment. A screen reader is a software application that attempts to identify and interpret what is being displayed on the screen. This interpretation is then re-presented to the user with text-to-speech, sound icons, or a Braille output device (Source: Wikipedia). Jaws is a widely used screen reader but it is a rather expensive tool. Laptops from Apple have a built-in function in their operative systems called "VoiceOver". Even if Apple-products are rather expensive to purchase, the built-in screen reader are free and maybe less expensive than a Windows-computer with Jaws.

Embedded voice and screen readers on smartphones and tablets

During the recent years smartphones and tablets have taken over much of the functionality earlier associated with the use of PCs.

  • Ipad and Iphone have “VoiceOver” too and the program can in many contexts replace Jaws.
  • Smartphones with the operating system Android have the embedded screen reader "TalkBack". Be aware of that the quality of “Talkback” differs in the different apps.
  1. Go to "Settings"
  2. Select "Availability"
  3. Select " VoiceOver"/"Talkback"
  4. Turn on "VoiceOver"/"Talkback". (On Android 4.0 you must go back and activate the touch exploration too)

Increase the font size and the contrast

Both iPhone and iPad and several Android phones have the built in ability to enlarge what you see on the screen, to increase the font size or increase the contrast.

Tixels replacing pixels

The Austrian company “Blitap” has developed a tablet which replaces the traditional touch-screen with a tactile display. Instead of visual pixels small physical bubbles (tixels) are raising from the screen. The screen has the capacity of 14 lines with braille and it can even display graphical content.

The tablet is divided into two parts where text and images are displayed as graphic representation on the lower touch-screen and as similar tactile representation on the upper screen.

A text to speech program allow the user to navigate on the ordinary touch-screen. Webpages as well as textfiles and PDF´s from USB memory sticks or SD-cards can be displayed on the tactile screen.

The “Blitap” was presented at the Consumer Electronics Show (CES) in January 2017. It is estimated to be available in the stores later this year and the price is expected to be around 2800 $.



Useful apps for Ipad, Iphone and Android phones

At Itunes some apps which can be of great help to people who are visually impaired or blind, can be downloaded.

  • ViA is an abbreviation for "Visually Impaired Apps". ViA has been designed to help identify apps that are useful for adults and children who are blind or have low vision, including those with additional disabilities. When using this app, you can easily do a free search or search different categories, and quickly get a list within your search fields with apps like other visually impaired people around the world have tried and later recommended. The app is a good starting point and it is totally free.

  • Color Reader's Voice tells the colour of an object. Put the iPhone on an object and touch the center of screen, Color Reader's voice tells name of color detected. The app recognize the colours white, light gray, gray, dark gray, black, red, orange, orange yellow, yellow, yellow green, green, cyan, blue, purple, magenta, red, brown and pink. The app is free of charge.

  • VoCal fuses a Dictaphone, Calendar and Alarm system into one. You simply speak your personal memo, set the time and date and you will be reminded in your voice. There is no need to type text! The price for this app will be around 1 USD.

  • Use your phone camera to take a picture of a text. Only a few seconds later the picture of the written text is converted into speech. The "TextGrabber + Translator" app make it happen. The price will be around 1 USD.

  • With Voice Dictation you can use your voice instead of typing. Touch the microphone dictation app, then say what you have to say, the iPhone converts your words into text. Use the dictation to write an email or an SMS messages, taking notes and more. Mostly this app is very good at recognizing words. If you speak clearly the app makes relatively few errors. The price: approximately 1 USD.

  • TapTapSee is designed to help the blind and visually impaired identify objects they encounter in their daily lives. Simply double tap the screen to take a photo of anything, at any angle, and hear the app speak the identification. The app is free of charge.

  • Be My Eyes is an app for Ipad/Iphone. Blind users can request help from a sighted person and the sighted users will then be called for help. As soon as the first sighted user accepts the request for help a live audio-video connection will be set up between the two and the sighted user can tell the blind person what she sees when the blind user points his phone at something using the rear-facing camera. The app is free of charge but the needed video connection is not free. It can be rather expencive tu use this app often.

  • Do you want to learn braille? Then Braille Driller is perfect. It is free and includes a review of the Braille alphabet and four activities of increasing difficulty to help become proficient with it. If you have an Adroid phone you can learn Braille with the "Braille" app. You can browse the Braille table, or take simple quiz for writing and reading Braille. It even support Korean Braille!

  • There are several apps for magnifying a text or picture using the camera in the phone. On Gooogle Play you can choose Magnifying Glassor other similar apps for Android phones. For Ipad and Iphone Magnifying Glass With Light is a proposal. If it is dark you can use the flash as a light. Some of the apps are free, while others cost a small amount.
