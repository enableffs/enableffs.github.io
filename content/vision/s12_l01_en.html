The blind basket maker


Zainab Mbuguni is a blind basker maker in Dar Es Salaam. She learned basketry before she got blind. In a house owned by Tanzania league of the blind she splits straw to long strips that will turn into curves. She produces braided curves for sale and earns a living with this business. Selling water to the community is another important income for her.

Zainab Mbuguni, Dar Es Salaam, basketry

My name is Zainab Mbuguni. I live in Kinondoni, D´salaam. My education is on the primary level. I did not pass my exams, so I learned to become a tailor. I started facing eye problems after I suffered from headache. That was in 1991. I went to the hospital in 1992, and they told me that I had to undergo surgery. In 1992 I had to remove one of my eyes. In 1994 I had to have another surgery, and the doctor that preformed my surgery said that I would only be able to see.

In 1995 I lost the ability to see. So, in 1995 I lost sight completely. I was sad and sorrowful, and I did not know what to do. But the doctor told me to go to an association for blind people, and that there were people who could tell me what to do there. In 1998 I joined the association who took me to an institution where I was thought to do different things. Since 1998-2000 I was at this institution where I was thought to do different things, including farming. I did not think I could to any kind of work, it was a bit stranger for me. But I knew basketry, because my mother thought me that before I got blind.

For some time, I did not know what to do. But when I joined the association, and when I got involved in basketry, I opened a kiosk where I sell different kind of items. Now I work hand in hand with this association. Later on, I thought that this kiosk did not pay enough. Because of the location it did not go well. I started selling water and other things, and then I moved to this new area. This is now my main duty, but there are a few challenges. The main one is the marked to sell my products. So, if I hear something from others I go there to sell my things.

How is this business, can you survive of this?
Yes, I live on this business, but it’s not easy. Sometimes I get very tired, it causes chest pain, and I don’t feel ok. Then I rest for a while and I come back later.

Have you got much support from family, the community or an organization?
Yes, my family has not left me alone, but they are not rich so they support me as best as they can. One in my family worked for the association, and told me about it. I have even seen that people that are blind that go to school sometimes perform better than people that are not. This was encouraging. I never despair, I just keep on struggling. This is something that you can’t do today and you get someone to buy your product tomorrow. You have to wait. But this is not the only thing I do, I do some other jobs as well.

What would you tell other blind people to do to get a meaningful job and a meaningful life?
I have met several people with visual impairment, I tell them not to despair, and to keep on moving. Not stay in one place waiting. I tell them to use their other senses, and use their words. They should rather get moving and try to make their own living, than to wait for others to help them.

Do you get any help for the government?
What kind of help?
Financial.
There is no money from the government for blind people.

She mentioned other business. What are they?
I sell water to the community. This is an important business for me.

Was she given a diagnose? What did they call the disease?
It was just a headache.

If you get hurt or something, and not able to work. What is your opportunity?
I don’t know how I would survive. That depends on what God decides for me. But I pray that I don’t have any more problems. It would be very difficult for me.

What difficulties have you met on the way?
The place where I live. It is only reachable if it not rains. If it’s raining, the soil get wet and slippery and I cannot go here. I have to ask people if it’s raining or not. If it has rained when I was here, I cannot get home. Then I have to go to my relatives. I have to get someone to drive me with a motorbike. If it rains when I’m home, I can’t get to work. If it rains when I’m at work I can’t get home.

Profession training

Every individual has a dream of what they want to be. It is not exceptional to persons with visual impairment. They also have their dreams in life- they aspire to be lawyers, journalists, school teachers and university lecturers, just to mention a few. The most powerful tool to enable individuals to achieve their dreams is providing them with quality education.
In East Africa, as it is in other many other countries, there are joint efforts to support provision of education to persons with visual impairment. Due to various reasons including poverty and social barriers, learners with visual impairment face barriers to equal learning opportunities, which may curtail their professional dreams in life.
The challenges of accessibility can be of two forms: first, access to education systems and secondly, access to curriculum content for learning.
Educational institutions which may enroll learners with visual impairment are limited. Hence, many of them may have limited access to higher education.

Teaching learners with visual impairment requires pedagogical skills and appropriate technology to facilitate learning. Likewise, the learners with visual impairment need appropriate supportive technology and facilities to enhance learning. Otherwise, limited access to learning may occur regardless of physical access to educational systems. learners with visual impairment experience difficulties in learning sciences, mathematics and any subjects which makes use of graphics representation due to lack of appropriate supportive teaching and learning resources.

Appropriate teaching and learning resources for enhancing learning, include:

  • Braillers
  • Braille embossers
  • Computers with installed speech software
  • Adaptive keyboards
  • Screen readers
  • Talking scientific calculators
  • Optical devices
