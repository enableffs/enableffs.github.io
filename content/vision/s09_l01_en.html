People with visual impairment may need strategies to know where they are and where they want to go. Different strategies may help them to walk safely so orientation and mobility are important.

Orientation

Orientation refers to the knowledge, skills and ability of a person with visual impairment to be able to know where he is or where he wants to go. Most people with visual impairment can do that by using their remaining senses.

How to get a mental picture of an unknown square



  1. Start in a corner.
  2. Trail the wall/edge to the next corner.
  3. Turn around and return half way back.
  4. Turn ninety degrees towards the square and move over the square to the other end.
  5. Turn ninety degrees again and move towards the corner.
  6. Turn ninety degrees once more and move towards the starting point.
  7. Turn ninety degrees and move a quarter of the whole length.
  8. Turn ninety degrees towards the square and move half way of the distance to the other side.
  9. Then turn ninety degrees again and move to the wall/edge.

Start in a corner.

Trail the wall.

Turn and return.

An unknown square is now discovered.

Mobility

Mobility refers to the ability of moving through the environment in a safe and easy way. Mobility devices like a long white cane, dog guides and other electronic travel aids may support the movement.

The importance of assistance

The white cane is one of the most important devices for the mobility of persons with visual impairment. Sometimes, especially in the East African region, these devices may not be easily accessible. Therefore, you will find people being guided by others, who are commonly referred to as sighted guides. It is also possible to make a long white cane from the locally available materials around. In schools, children with visual impairment ma y have a friend who assists them.
