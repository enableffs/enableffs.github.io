Louis Braille (1809-1852) lost his sight in an accident when he was three years old. In 1821, at the age of sixteen, he invented a system of reading and writing for use by persons who are blind or visually impaired. The system in known asÂ Braille.

At that time, persons who were blind read elevated Latin letters. These letters took time to read and it was complicated to produce such a text. Today braille remains known worldwide. Braille is written in different languages in the world including English, French, Kiswahili, Hindi amongst others. Braille codes have been developed in mathematics, music, science and computer paving way for learners with visual impairment to access education to the highest levels.

The braille system

Braille consist of cells which contain six elevated points which constitute the cell, two points in the width and the three points in the height. Different combinations of the points create 63 characters: letters, punctuation, numbers and words. From above the points in the left row is numbered 1-2-3 and the right row 4-5-6.

Contractions

For making communication more effective and less timeconsuming, Braille is divided in different contractions, which means that the most common words and combination of letters are represented as specialized abbreviations.

The contractions are defined in different levels:

  • Grade one Braille with no contractions. Uncontracted braille comprises the alphabet, numbers, punctuation and some composition symbols. All words are spelled out in full, with no shorterned versions.
  • Grade one and a half Braille with about 50 contractions
  • Grade two Braille, uses a number of âcontractionsâ which are basically used to make words shorter. There are 189 contractions in total. Most literature that is embossed in braille is written in contracted Braille.
  • Grade three Braille have many hundreds of contractions

What Braille is used for?

With braille it is possible to convert different materials into a format that can be accessible by people with visual impairment. Students can also use Braille to prepare their work and print in a format that is accessible to sighted teachers. Therefore Braille can accommodate the needs of visually impaired students.

If affordable it is possible to get Braille textbooks, paperless Braille e-books and writing devices.

Embossed Braille takes up more space than print, so producing a shorthand version of Braille helps make Braille literature less bulky.
