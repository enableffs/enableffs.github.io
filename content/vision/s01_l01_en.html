A blind person is an ordinary person - just blind.

Vision can simply refer to the ability to see. This ability is enabled by the sensory system. The sensory system is a part of the nervous system, and is responsible for processing vision sensory information. What the eye sees is what we refer to as sensory information.

In the process of seeing the information is processed through a visual system which includes:

  • the sense organ (eye)
  • the receptors-rods and cones
  • the whole process of visual pathways which starts from eye to the brain.

A person whose vision is so severely affected to the extent of not being able to see is referred to as blind.

Courtesy rules of blindness

During verbal interaction

  • You do not need to raise your voice while conversing
  • Do not address an adult person who is blind as a child
  • Do not ask other people a question blind people could answer. E.g. What does he want? What is her name?

Mobility

He may use a long cane to walk independently; he may also take your arm for sighted guidance.

  • Let her decide for herself.
  • Do not grab her arm, let her take yours instead.
  • the person who is blind will take a help step behind you to anticipate potholes, steps and other barriers.
  • Guide him to a chair by placing his palm on the back or seat of the chair.

Presence

  • Announce your presence whenever you enter or leave a room where there is a blind person.
  • Speak when you enter.
  • Introduce him to others including children.
  • Let him know if there is a cat or dog in the house or elsewhere.
  • Prompt him whenever extending a hand for greetings.

Precautions

  • Any door left partially open is a hazard to the blind person.
  • Rooms, windows, cabinets, drawers should be left either completely open or completely closed.
