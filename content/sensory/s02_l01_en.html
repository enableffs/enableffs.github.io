Inclusion

Inclusion is the practice of educating where all learners are in the same school and classes alongside their peers. Learners with different abilities participate fully as equally valued and unique individuals. The Salamanca Statement and Framework for Action on Special Needs Education (1994) is probably the most significant international document that has ever appeared in connection to special needs education. It argues that:

Regular schools with inclusive orientation are creating welcoming communities, building an inclusive society, achieving education for all, and are the most effective means of combating discriminatory attitudes.

Unesco´s Guidelines for Inclusion (2005) states:

Education must be viewed as facilitative in everyone’s human development and functionality, regardless of barriers of any kind, physical or otherwise. Therefore, disability of any kind cannot be a disqualifier.

According to these guidelines, four key elements have tended to conceptualize inclusion:

  1. Inclusion is a process.
    Inclusion has to be seen as a never-ending search to find better ways of responding to diversity.
  2. Inclusion is concerned with the identification and removal of barriers.
    Collecting and evaluating information from a wide variety of sources in order to plan for improvements in policy and practice.
  3. Inclusion is about the presence, participation and achievement of all students.
    Where children are educated and how punctually they attend, the quality of their experiences whilst they are there and the outcomes of learning across the curriculum.
  4. Inclusion involves a particular emphasis on those groups of learners who may be at risk of marginalization, exclusion or underachievement.
    It´s a moral responsibility to ensure that those groups are carefully monitored and that the necessary steps are taken.

What inclusion is about:

  • welcoming diversity
  • benefiting all learners, not only targeting the excluded
  • children in school who may feel excluded
  • providing equal access to education or making certain provisions for certain categories of children without excluding them

Is inclusion always promising?

Uganda:
Francis Kanubi is the headmaster at a special school for the blind near Kampala in Uganda. Listen to his nuanced reflections about segregation and inclusion

I may not say that inclusive education has totally failed in Uganda, but I think it was not planned for by government when it was been taken up. I think they just took it up hazardly without preparing for it. For example, we have very many blind children here who are brought from inclusive schools, where a child was taken up by a school and attended primary one to primary seven, and eventually when he or she reach primary seven, she cannot be allowed to sit her primary leaving examination because through her primary school, she has not learned how to read and write, - Braille for example. In the school where such a school is the teacher don´t know Braille. The teachers don´t have equipment, for example slate, for example braille paper. So instead of learning a child how to read and write and maybe how to do mathematics in a Braille format, they just teach them verbally and the child learns by memory. And by the time the child reach primary seven, because he or she has been answering questions verbally, and being questioned verbally, and the child answers verbally, and the teacher write for him or her the answers the child has given. When the child has reached primary seven the examiners don´t allow something like that happen. That´s the biggest disadvantages in an inclusive setting in Uganda. So by the time the child is brought back here at the primary level, we take back the child to primary five to learn Braille, and those are many years lost, which is a very big inconvenience to a child.

The headmaster at the school for the blind has a reflected view on the pros and cons of inclusion. He sees some advantages with special schools for the blind. These include giving time to and focusing on, for example, activities for daily living (ADL) and mobility. However, he also knows the disadvantages with that kind of segregation.

Segregation

Segregation is when the learners are in a special school or institutions where the other learners have the same or other difficulties. In such segregated educational settings, he learners often tend to live a separate life from the life outside school

Even if declarations are signed and professional and political will is present, it takes a long time before implementation takes place. This is also the case with inclusion. Occasionally, such processes completely stop, and sometimes authorities find more easy-to-win solutions.

However, it is important to be aware that inclusion is not:

  • reforms of special education alone, but reform of both the formal and non-formal education system
  • responding only to diversity, but also improving the quality of education for all learners
  • special schools but perhaps additional support to students within the regular school system
  • meeting the needs of children with disabilities only
  • meeting one child’s needs at the expense of another child

Francis Kanubi also sees some disadvantages of inclusion

I think the biggest disadvantage I can say is not sharing with their sighted counterparts, whom they were sharing with when they were still in the previous school, where they were learning together side by side the sighted children. So previously when they were in the mainstream schools they were making friends with the sighted children, the sighted children could read for them, and when it was time for sports, sighted children would be the ones to guide them to athletic championships. And the teachers in that particular school where also getting used to blind children, which they were note used to before. So that is the biggest disadvantages, that our children missing that relationship, that kind of socialization with the sighted children in that school.

Integration

What about a step between inclusion and segregation, integration?

Integration is when the learner is being placed in mainstream education settings with some adaptations and resources. The structures are pre-existing and the attitudes are not changed.

Inclusion is based on knowledge.
Integration is based on understanding.
Segregation is based on acceptance.
Exclusion is based on denial.
