Hearing impairment may affect an individual in many ways. The most important are:

  • functional communication
  • social and emotional development
  • academic performance
  • economic status

Functional communication

One of the main effects of hearing loss is on the individual’s ability to communicate with others. Language development is often delayed in children with hearing impairment.
However, when opportunities are provided for people with hearing loss to communicate, they can participate on an equal basis with others. The communication may be through spoken language combined with lip reading or through sign language.

CODA - Children of Deaf Parents

Approximately 90% of the children born to deaf parents are hearing. Since sign language is the preferred way of communication in families where the parents are deaf and the children are hearing, the network around them may be concerned about that the children get too little spoken language stimulation. In today's society, it is usually an unfounded concern.
The fact that the child learns both sign and oral language should be viewed as a benefit. Multilingualism is positive for cognitive development, including in terms of developing concepts and creativity.

Read more about Second Language Acquisition.

Children who develop multiple languages simultaneously will at times mix languages. However, at any given time, it is the dominant language in the conversation that will dictate the pattern of language mixing. Additionally, it’s quite natural that the child will learn a linguistic term for an item in one language before the other. If the child alternates between the languages is called code-switching and there is no cause for concern. On the contrary, it testifies to a high degree of mastery of the language when they are able to utilize their entire language skills.

Social conventions

Hearing children of deaf parents grow up in homes with visual communication. They are accustomed to other forms of contact more than hearing children. Children who are accustomed to visual communication from an early age, often need eye contact when they talk with others. Lack of eye contact may cause a child to ignore or not to perceive messages. At home children are used to having contact by touching each other lightly on the arm, stamping the floor, blinking the lights, or tapping the table.

This may be unusual, but it is not - it's just a little difference. Parents cannot always explain what sounds mean, here the school can contribute both correction and explanation.
Parents, school staff and other residents in the child's network can reflect on and discuss different social conventions together with the child. This will be an important contribution, so the child can practise listening without seeing, and be conscious of the social conventions and sounds that are culturally accepted in the two cultures in which they live.

Effect on academic performance

Hearing loss can have significantly adverse effects on the academic performance of children. This is because the child may not hear some of the oral information presented by the teacher, and may consequently lag behind. The child has to rely on written instruction, in the absence of an interpreter. She/he may lack the opportunity for incidental learning that their hearing peers enjoy.

Deaf parents may well help their children with homework, but like parents in general, there may be some parents who experience certain subjects as difficult. Many deaf people have the local oral and written language as their second language and may have a strained relationship with it. If the parents have little or no residual hearing, it can be hard to hear children in homework. Here, older siblings or others in the family networks can help, or an agreements may be reached with another person to support the child with homework.

Effects on social and emotional development

Limited access to services and exclusion from participation in day to day activities and communication can have significant impact on everyday life, causing feelings of loneliness, isolation and frustration, particularly among older people with hearing loss. If a person with congenital hearing loss has not been given the opportunity to learn sign language or other forms of communication as a child, he or she may feel excluded from social interaction.

Effects on economic status

In developing countries, children with hearing impairment, including deafness, rarely receive any formal education. Adults with hearing loss also have a much higher unemployment rate. Among those who are employed, a higher percentage of people with hearing loss are in the lower grades of employment compared with the general workforce. Improving access to education and vocational rehabilitation services, and raising awareness especially among employers about the needs of people with hearing loss, would decrease unemployment rates among this group and hence improve their economic status.

In addition to the economic effects of hearing loss at an individual level, hearing loss substantially affects social and economic development in families, communities and countries.
