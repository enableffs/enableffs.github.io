Communication is a big challenge

The biggest challenge for people with hearing loss is communication. Although parts of the sound are missed out, it is still possible to perceive that somebody is talking. When some sounds are missing or weak, it may be difficult to understand the exact meaning of what is being said.

Therefore, it may have consequences on the child's language development.

It is always important to create good listening and communication situations regardless of the degree and nature of the hearing loss.

Children with hearing loss

Hearing loss in children does not only make it difficult for them to communicate with other people; it also slows down, or even prevents altogether, their learning. A great deal of what children learn comes from what they (over) hear. They hear their family members talking and they learn. They hear people in the community talking and they learn. They hear other children talking and they learn. They hear the radio or television and they learn. All this is in addition to what is said to them.
Children who have a hearing loss may have challenges learning.
While children with mild hearing loss miss out some information; those who have a severe hearing impairment miss out nearly everything. So they need very special help in order to learn.

Source: Deafness: A Guide for Parents, Teachers and Community Workers. UNESCO

Up to 5 of every 1000 infants are born with or develop disabling hearing loss in early childhood.

Unilateral Hearing Loss (UHL) and the development of a child

Having unilateral hearing loss will mean having a hearing loss in one ear and normal hearing in the other.

Hi! Kick the ball to me!

For people with unilateral hearing loss it is not easy to locate the sound. From which direction did the sound come?

  • Localization is important when listening in groups of people. It helps direct us to who is talking at any moment. If a child takes longer to identify who is talking, he or she may miss some of the message.
  • Localization is also important for safety. If a child is near the street, he or she may have trouble localizing the source of hooting.

Sound localization is one of the challenges related to unilateral hearing loss.

Other challenges can be:

  • Evaluation of the distance to the audio input.
  • Speech intelligibility from the side with hearing loss
  • Speech perception and communication in noisy environments. Two normal hearing ears help us to filter out noise to better hear speech.

When there is a big difference between hearing in the left and right ear, the processing of audio signals from the side with hearing loss can often be reduced . One may find that the audio signals coming into the side with hearing loss , are not used . This is a dynamic that a person cannot control themselves.

Unilateral hearing loss (UHL) and the development of the child

A child who is in the process of developing language, cannot make a guess or "fill in the gaps" about what he or she didn't understand.

However, a child's unilateral hearing loss will not hinder him or her from learning to talk even if some children with UHL have been found to have delays in their speech and language development.

How to help children with UHL

Adults can help children with UHL by being good speech and language models to the children and being aware of the listening environment. The following are some ways of helping children with UHL:

  • Be aware of where your child's "normal" hearing ear is facing.
  • Let the child face the person speaking or ensure that his or her normal hearing ear is facing the person.
  • Make eye contact when speaking to your child.
  • Talk about your daily routine.
  • Use repetition and expand your child's vocabulary by introducing new words.
  • Get your child's attention before talking to him or her. Be sure the child does not miss any of the intended messages.
  • Help your child localize sound if he or she appears to be having difficulty.
  • Look for cues that your child understands what you are saying.
  • Raise your voice slightly and face him or her when you are at a distance.
  • Don´t give instructions from another room.

Early Intervention

Early intervention is a comprehensive set of services that are provided to children with hearing loss and their families. These services are aimed at minimizing the effect of this disability and to enhance their development. Early intervention services are crucial to minimizing the impact of hearing loss on a child’s development and educational achievements. Early intervention programmes can improve the linguistic and educational outcomes for the child.

Early intervention also helps families of children with hearing loss to make decisions about their child such as:

  • The communication approach to be used
  • Educational placement for the child
  • The hearing technology to be used for the child

Infants and young children with severe hearing loss should be given the opportunity to learn sign language along with their families.

Conditions for developing communication skills

Children who are deaf and or hard hearing children often have a poor conceptualization of information amd difficulty to reflect on their own and others' feelings and experiences. The result can be social difficulties in making friends. Due to lack of basic concepts many children are struggling academically at school. For getting a good language development, sign language environment is absolutely essential for these children. It is a precondition for social adjustment and for good mental health later in life to create an inner voice for thinking and understanding. It is essential that parents and children get the opportunity to communicate well right from the start. That's when the connection between parents and children is formed and the interplay between them should start.
For hard of hearing children, early communication with sign language in addition to speech is an advantage for the development of spoken language and concepts and has good impact on later reading and writing skills.

Early detection and intervention are crucial to minimizing the impact of hearing loss on a child’s development and educational achievements. In infants and young children with hearing loss, early identification and management through infant hearing screening programmes can improve the linguistic and educational outcomes for the child.

Children with deafness should be given the opportunity to learn sign language along with their families.
