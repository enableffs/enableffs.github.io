Right to education

Children with hearing impairment need and have a right to education. At present many children with hearing impairment do not have that opportunity. Parents, teachers and other stakeholders should ensure that these children access and participate in education.

Many deaf children are not offered formal education. How will their future be?

Different forms of educational provision

The society becomes more and more independent if citizens are well educated. Hearing-impaired citizens who are educated are a resource for the community which can be utilized for national development. There are different forms of schooling that have been established for deaf children.

Educational provision for children with hearing impairment can be grouped in the following categories “special schooling”, “special units in mainstream schools”  and “inclusive education”. Each of them has some advantages and some disadvantages.

"Special schools" are segregated schools only for deaf children. They have either day or boarding provisions.

Advantages:

  • The children have other deaf children to play with and to learn with.
  • There are more teachers and a bigger range of subjects and skills can be taught.
  • There may be more specialist equipment and books available.
  • Often there are deaf adults doing many of the other jobs in the school – cleaning, cooking, house-parenting and hence become role models to the children.
  • It is likely that they will have many deaf friends when they leave school.
  • They have no difficulty in communicating with each other outside lesson times.
  • There is no danger of the children feeling the isolation that deaf children often feel if they are with people who have difficulty in communicating with them.

Disadvantages:

  • The children may not learn how to live in society with many different people. They may find it hard to settle after they leave school and their friends.
  • Residential schools are very costly. In many countries it is too expensive to build enough schools for all the deaf children.
  • Parents may find it expensive to take the children home for the holidays and take them back when the term starts. They may be tempted to leave the children at school without visiting them.
  • The children may lose touch with their families.
  • The children do not often meet hearing children
  • The children may miss the activities of school during the holidays. They may not have friends in the local community.

When the deaf children are placed in a class of hearing children it is usually called “mainstreaming”. This is a placement with difficulties unless there is some special help and attention for the deaf child.

  • The child may not understand what the teacher is saying and doing.
  • The other children may exclude or make fun of the deaf child.

If all the children, hearing, deaf, physically or intellectually disabled, blind, go to school together it is often called “inclusive education”.

The main advantage of inclusive education is that it provides real-life situation.

But it does need teachers to be able to understand and adapt to many different children and their needs.

Inclusion of children with hearing impairment

Children with hearing impairmentcan learn together with their hearing peers in ordinary schools. They only need support to be able to achive this.
Rose is the only deaf pupil at Karanda Primary School in Kenya and is studying together with hearing peers. Selene is both her classmate and her interpreter.


Rose has been included in ordinary classes from grade 1.

Selene has been her interpreter for many years.

The teacher is worried about Rose Sofie's future schooling.



Listen to the interview with Edith Aceno Wambore, teacher at Karanda Primary School.

About Rose

Rose is one of our girls here. She came here in class one and we realized that she was hearing impaired when she was brought. We had to take her historical background then after that she was given the right placement in class one where she interacted with the other learners. Through difficulty madam Rose managed to have Selena as her interpreter. Madam used to interpret a little bit and then she decided to give her a peer, at least a child of her age that could help her with interpretation. So we discovered that children learn faster than the elders. And they went together to class two until now. She never repeated a class. Every year she is promoted to next level and now she is in grade eight.

How is it to have a hearing impaired child in the class?

No it is okay because at least it is a way of enabling the community members to communicate with. You know children are acters or agents of change and by having a such a child in class here. When they meet other children who also are hearing impaired, at least they can communicate to them because I always discovered that most of these special need children, they kind of feel withdrawn because they dont have friends around. But the fact that we have them here at school have enabled other community members to accept them. Even the parents who used to hide their children at home are now coming to us and requesting to put the children in school. We even have worse cases and other cases. From at least we are going to meet these children, we are getting to be with them. They are being accepted by the community members, they are being accepted by their parents and the entire community, even the teachers who are not trained.

What about the other children?

They do communicate, but not as much as Selene does. You know everything is developing interest. And Selene because she have been trained at that earlier level. The others equally can only communicate with her, not as fluent as Selene does, but at least the normal way of communication with deaf. When Selene is not around at least she finds a few she can interact with. Well they stay two worlds apart, Selene stays down, down, down. And she stays the other side.
When she going home she moves with the others who are not Selene, and Selene also moves to the other child.

Disadvantages for the interpreter The only disadvantages we really talked to her about is when she interprets she does not want to move her lips. She finds difficulty in moving her lips. I think when she started doing it, maybe that was were there was some faults. Maybe the teacher didnt train her to move her lips, and maybe by training and coming up she will be used to just doing it the way the other girl will also do it. But otherwise we have always tried to talk to her every day about moving her lips. But it is a bit difficult interpreting the teacher is teaching, you also want to get into whatever you been taught. But she does it perfectly and she performs very well in class.

Is it good for the girl to be an interpreter?

She don´t loose her friends, she still have a friend. The one she moves with to her home. The one she plays around with here in school. But they really dont walk together the two of them. If you go outside there you will find her mingling with her class members together. And she does what she does. She does her homework, she does everything like any other child, no special treatment.

Is the interpreter volunteer or pushed into it?

When Sofie came to school they used to sit together and they were friends. And you know for those young children making friendship something that is quite easy. So because of that, the Madam introduced her into it, because she used to fumble a lot. Madam would see her trying to sign a lot for her. Like now we have a deaf boy who came last year and you know in that class we still dont have so many who can interpret. But you find that nobody has pushed anybody. They just want to come out individually, to help him, to help this particular boy. They want him to be associated with that particular hearing impaired boy.

Is this a way of handling the situation you will recommend?

I wish we had interpreters who are paid. I wish we would have, but by us having such an interpreter, it mean that this child would only communicate with the interpreter. What about the peers? These children have to learn with their peers, they have to grow up together, they have to fight, they have to play, they have to do everything together. Supposing you employ me am interpreter at my age and such a young child. You know, it wouldn´t really be. If he does it with a peer i think it is so much better than doing it with somebody who is not at their age.

How do you help learners who are deaf?

Normally, like you can see that we already have a challenge. I told you we have a number of teachers who are trained in special needs. All of us did inclusive and in inclusive, we have almost all the areas that we covered. So we have those who have developed an interest, like in this situation madam who has developed interest in sign language. And at her free time during games she calls Sofie and even us, we call her and talk to her and give her extra work that may be she didn't get during class time. It has to be done. But for class work time she has to do.

What will happen when they complete class eight?

We are worried about that. We are worried about that and we are even thinking of them going to the same school. But the problem is: Yes they will go to the same school – what about the there after? That is the main worry. We handle this till like we handled before. They completed class eight, after that, there is nobody to pick up from there. And some of us wish we should be secondary teachers too. We bring those children, bring them back to school and may be we should start up a vocational training center. We long for that. So the such children who complete, we bring them here. We even had a visually impaired girl who performed very well. She went to Ahero Girls unfortunately somewhere in between somebody impregnated her and she disappeared. She dropped out of school.

Do Selene and Sofie normally stay together?


They are normally separated. When during holidays they are never together. And we can find other days where they are never together. So during this time when they are not together, they are both living their own lives. That is the headache. May be a boarding school were we don´t go home. So they are already that. There are times where they are not together. So our worry is after class eight. That is our worry.
But to me I wouldn´t mind if maybe Sofie goes to a special school at that level. Because already at her tender age she has come up in an inclusive set up. So at that age maybe for one up to form four. If she goes to maybe a special school she still will be accepted by the community because she is grown up with them. My only worry is if she drops out of that level. For what´s next after dropping out. Is she going to be a mother and be living in a terrible community where some people are misusing such a young girl.

The importance of sign language

Sign language is important in the education of children with hearing impairment. They should therefore be supported to learn this language. Uganda school for the deaf is a special school where the pupils learn sign language as their mother tongue and English or other local language as their second language. It is impossible to use sign language with spoken language simultaneously; that is why sign language is still a challenge to the learners' education and parents'.

Examination provisions

The current National Primary Examination in Uganda and Kenya demands the Deaf to be fluent in English before they are graded in a given subject, which situation ignores the fact that, learning any second language is influenced by ones mother tongue.

In their examination regulations "The Uganda National Examinations Board" allows deaf candidates to be guided by sign language interpreters and readers who are expected to interpret or voice record candidates responses.

Nevertheless, as a result, majority of the deaf adolescents end up failing exams and eventually dropping out of school.

Inclusion of learners who are hard of hearing in an ordinary class

If there is a student who is hard of hearing in your class, you should be aware of these adaptations of the classroom environment:

  • The sitting arrangement should enable children to see one another
  • Ensure that light falls on the face of the person who is talking, backlight should be avoided
  • Be sure you get the child´s attention before starting the communication
  • Use simple vocabulary
  • Ensure that your face is clearly seen
  • Use various communication modes
  • learners should speak one at a time
