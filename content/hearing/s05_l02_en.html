What is sign language?

Sign language is a visual and gestural language based on the use of hands, eyes, face, mouth and body.

Here are some of the letters in Kenyan Sign Language which is used by over half of the country's estimated 600,000 deaf population.

Wherever communities of deaf people exist, sign languages have been developed. A common misconception is that all sign languages are the same worldwide.Each country generally has its own, native sign language, and some have more than one.

In Kenya for instance, there are some dialect differences between Kisumu and Mombasa. At the same time, sign languages may share similarities to each other.

The development of sign language in Uganda

The development of sign in Uganda stems from the coopration between Kyambogo University and Uganda National Association of the Deaf. The two Institutions coraborated in training teachers and other people in sign language. The training is supported by the development of the sign language manual and Uganda Sign Language Dictionary

Dr. Sam Lutalo-Kiingi is an Ugandan sign language lecturer in the Department of Special Needs Education at Kyambogo University. And he is the first deaf African to undertake a PhD in linguistics.

I was born in Uganda at a time when education for the deaf still was at a low level. My mother wanted me to have a good education so I was sent to Kenya to live with my uncle and to attend a school were I was thought sign language. So my first language was sign language. But still it was really difficult for me to communicate with my family and other hearing persons, because they did not know sign language. I continued to work hard, and my mother helped me to learn to read and write English. However the communication with hearing people still was really difficult, as it was for many other deaf people in Uganda.

The key that opened our doors

In the 1990’s a group of deaf people started an advocacy to improve their lives and Uganda National Association for the Deaf grew bigger. At about this time sign language was recognized as a language in the constitution, which was really important for the deaf community. This also meant that sign language was accepted in schools. To have an own language made it easier to communicate, and it also made deaf people feel proud.

"The hearing community and the deaf community came together though an interpreter"

Even though the communication became easier between deaf people with an own language, it was still some challenges communicating with the hearing. And when I met Prossy (Proscovia Suubi Nantongo, Lecturer, Kyambogo University) and started to teach her sign language and to interpret it was a real breakthrough. Using her as an interpreter made it so much easier to communicate with the hearing. So the association continued to carrying out programs and demanding services for the deaf. And when using interpreters the work was less difficult.

My advices for deaf people

I was lucky to have a mother who gave me the support and the opportunity to have so much education. In 2012 I was the first deaf African to finish my PhD in linguistics. But I met a lot of challenges on my way.

My advice is to work hard and be patient. You have to work hard and look for channels were you would attend something that will make you better. Sometimes it is difficult, but if you have a goal you can achieve it. So many things can come your way, and prevent you from achieving your goal, but I had my family and council, and I asked for help on my way. And here I am now, achieving my goal.

Secondly, some deaf people are born with hearing parents. They have some other challenges, and some won’t accept their deaf child. I think the number one thing is acceptance! And also parents learning sign language is very important.

My experience is that I can’t communicate very well with my father because he doesn’t now sign language. I always have to look for an interpreter to communicate with him.

I urge parent to learn sign language so they can talk to their children. It’s so much easier to communicate with the same language.

The development of sign language in Tanzania

The development of sign language in Tanzania is a quite different story from the development of sign language in Uganda. In Tanzania sign language is not the main way of communicate. How come?

The deaf learners were told to speak, were forced to be able to speak to become normal



Thomas Elias Shayo
Sign language interpreter, University of Dar Es Salaam

Listen to the interview with Thomas Elias Shayo:

It is historical. In Tanzania, education for the deaf has been provided by NGOs and churches from way back in 1960s. So the first school for the deaf which was supported from by roman-catholic church in Tabora region, was teaching the learners in speech, we call it oralism. So the deaf learners were told to speak, were forced to be able to speak to become normal. And the philosophy behind it was that; signing was not good for deaf people, because if they sign other people will not understand them. Their families will not understand them, so the church decided that the best way to teach them is to speak, but unfortunately they could not teach them to hear. And that was a big problem.

We face challenges because deafness in East-African countries is not inherited. Deafness comes by disease. Most of the deaf cases in Tanzania are by diseases, like meningitis, malaria and other things. Now if there is only one deaf person in a family of lets say ten-twenty people, this deaf person becomes isolated. Now, sign language is the best way, but who will teach sign language to all this family members so they can communicate with this one person. It looks like a very big job and very expensive. So when this deaf child goes to school so it natural that when they meet and they understand that we are all deaf, they start signing. But their signing is not standardized, it depends on the school. We use to have ten schools for the deaf in the whole country with a population of almost fifty millions.
But now it is changing because the government is opening up units in all primary schools, many primary schools. At least we are having some deaf communities in these schools and they develop their own signing because there is no one to teach them sign language, there is no one to copy from. So we have a diverse vocabulary of signing from all over the country. Sign language is a problem, it is the best way for the deaf, but it a problem; where do you go to learn sign language?

The thing is, there are no qualified interpreters in Tanzania because there is no place, no college in this country where you can go and get a bachelor's degree in sign language or even a diploma or even a certificate.

Thomas E. Shayo often helps other Tanzanian interpreters with signing specific words. The interpretation is video recorded with a smartphone and distributed through social media.

Introducing sign language to preschool-children

Around twenty preschool children and five teachers crouch in a circle outdoor in the schoolyard. They are playing a game. One boy runs outside the circle and suddenly he claps another child on her head.
One teacher says: That one. Go.
The boy sits down and the girl starts running. After half a round she taps another boy on his head. He starts running and the kid is laughing. He seems very happy. The next activity is a teacher who stand in the middle of the circle doing something which look like a morning exercise. She jumps up and down clapping her hands over her head. The children stand in the circle and imitate the teachers movement.
The teacher says: Ok, now I'm going to pick one to come and do it for us. You are going to do the same there.
A child replaces the teacher and the exercise continues.
The teacher says: When I'm introducing a new activity, obviously, first it's me to do it. Then they see what I've done and copy it. They follow what I do and do the same. But first I explain to them in sign language.
A new activity take place. The children stand in a line.
In front of them the teacher explains, both verbally and with sign language: Look at me, I am going to clap, and then you run. I clap you run. Have you heard? Ok.
Two boys are squatting in a start position ready to run.
The teacher says: Look at me.
The teacher claps her hands and the children run as fast as they can.
The teacher says: Since they don't know these signs well, we sometimes do gestures first.
A new pair of children start running. The other children and teachers cheer on the competitors.
The teacher says: After that they learn the real signs. After learning the real signs they do the activities themselves.
The children are sitting in the shade of a tree side by side in a row. In front of them teacher try to get the childrens attention.
The teacher says: Look here. Look at me. Eyes here.
She is showing a leaf when she says: This is a leaf.
Then she shows the children the sign for leaf.
She says: This one is a leaf.
The children imitate the sign the teacher make with their hands.
She says: Have you all seen? Good.
In an interview under a big tree the teacher says: We normally teach the basics in sign language. Like how to sign a cup, a ball or water. We teach the children the basics in different areas. In those areas there are some competances, some learning activities, learning development. That is what we teach.

In summary, first the children imitate the teachers movement and copy them.
Then they learn new concepts and signs through pysical acticities.
New signs are presented in different representations;
- as physical objects,
- as drawings and
- as written words.
