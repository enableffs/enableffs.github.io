Louis Braille (1809-1852) lost his sight in an accident when he was three years old. In 1821, sixteen year old, he invented a system of reading and writing for use by the blind or visually impaired. The system in known as braille.

At that time blind read elevated Latin letters. These letters were slowly to read and it was complicated to produce such a text. Today braille remains known worldwide.

The braille system

Braille consist of cells which contain six elevated points which constitute the cell, two points in the width and the three points in the hight. Different combinations of the points create 63 characters: letters, punctuation, numbers and notes. From above the points in the left row is numbered 1-2-3 and the right row 4-5-6.

The top four points, 1-2-4-5, create the letters between a and j.

If point 3 is added the next ten charachters, k-l-m-n-o-p-q-r-s-t are created.

If point 3 and 6 are added, the charachters u-v-x-y-z are created. For the charachter w the points are 2-4-5-6.

Numbers

The symbols for the numbers 1 to 9 are similar to the symbols for the letters a to i except from a prefix.

Punctuation

The prefix symbol, "number follow", is added in front of the number. A capital letter is created with the prefix symbol "capital".

Contractions

For making communication more effectively and less timeconsuming, Braille is divided in different contractions which means that the most common words and combination of letters are represented as specialized abbreviations.

The contractions are defined in different levels:

  • Grade one braille with no contractions. Uncontracted braille comprises the alphabet, numbers, punctuation and some composition symbols. All words are spelled out in full, with no shorterned versions.
  • Grade one and a half braille with about 50 contractions
  • Grade two braille, uses a number of “contractions” which are basically used to make words shorter. There are 189 contractions in total. Most literature that is embossed in braille is written in contracted braille.
  • Grade three braille have many hundreds of contractions

What braille is used for

With braille it is possible to convert different materials into a format that can be accessible by people with visual impairment. Either students can also use braille to prepare their work and print in a format that is accessible to sighted teachers. Therefore braille can accommodate the needs of visually impaired students.

If affordable it is possible to get braille textbooks, paperless braille e-books and writing devices.

Embossed braille takes up more space than print, so producing a shorthand version of braille helps make braille literature less bulky.

The braille version of J.K Rowlings’ “Harry Potter and the Order of the Phoenix” runs to 13 volumes!
