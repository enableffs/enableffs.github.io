The biggest challenge for people with hearing loss is communication. Although parts of the sound falls out, it is still possible to perceive somebody is talking. But when some sounds are missing or weak, it may be difficult to understand the exact meaning of what is being said.

Other people may have difficulty understanding this, for example it may have consequences for the child's language development.

It is always important to create good listening and communication situations regardless of the degree and nature of the hearing loss.

Children with hearing loss

Hearing loss in children does not only make it difficult for them to communicate with other people; it also slows down, or even prevents altogether, their learning. A great deal of what children learn comes from what they (over) hear. They hear their family members talking and they learn. They hear people in the community talking and they learn. They hear other children talking and they learn. They hear the radio or television and they learn. All this is in addition to what is said to them.
Children who have a hearing loss miss out on all this learning.
Even children who are only a little bit hearing impaired miss out; children who are very hearing impaired miss out nearly everything. So they need very special help in order to learn.

Source: Deafness: A Guide for Parents, Teachers and Community Workers. UNESCO

Up to 5 of every 1000 infants are born with or develop disabling hearing loss in early childhood

Early Intervention

Early intervention is a comprehensive set of services that provided to children with hearing loss and their families in order to minimize the effect of this disability and to enhance their development. Early intervention services are crucial to minimizing the impact of hearing loss on a child’s development and educational achievements. Early intervention programmes can improve the linguistic and educational outcomes for the child.

Early intervention also helps families of children with hearing loss to make decisions about their child such as:

  • The communication approach to be used
  • Educational placement for the child
  • The hearing technology to be used for the child

In infants and young children with hearing loss, children with deafness should be given the opportunity to learn sign language along with their families.

Conditions for developing communication skills

Deaf and hearing-impaired children often have a poor conceptual framework and difficulty to reflect on their own and others' feelings and experiences. The result can be can social difficulties and difficulties in making friends. Because of lack of basic concepts many children are struggling academically at school. For getting a good language development sign language environment is absolutely essential for these children. It is a precondition for social adjustment and for good mental health later in life to create an inner voice for thinking and understanding. It is essential that parents and children get the opportunity to communicate well right from the start. That's when the connection between parents and children formed and the interplay between them should start.
For hard of hearing children early communication with sign language in addition to speech is an advantage for the development of spoken language and concepts and have good impact on later reading and writing skills.

Early detection and intervention are crucial to minimizing the impact of hearing loss on a child’s development and educational achievements. In infants and young children with hearing loss, early identification and management through infant hearing screening programmes can improve the linguistic and educational outcomes for the child.

Identification of hearing loss

Pre-school, school and occupational screening for ear diseases and hearing loss is an effective tool for early identification and management of hearing loss. People with hearing loss can benefit from the use of hearing devices, such as hearing aids, cochlear implants, and other assistive devices. They may also benefit from speech therapy, aural rehabilitation and other related services. However, global production of hearing aids meets less than 10% of global need and less than 3% of developing countries’ needs. The lack of availability of services for fitting and maintaining these devices, and the lack of batteries are also barriers in many low-income settings. Making properly-fitted, affordable hearing aids and cochlear implants and providing accessible follow-up services in all parts of the world will benefit many people with hearing loss. People who develop hearing loss can learn to communicate through development of lip-reading skills, use of written or printed text, and sign language. Teaching in sign language will benefit children with hearing loss, while provision of captioning and sign language interpretation on television will facilitate access to information. Officially recognizing national sign languages and increasing the availability of sign language interpreters are important actions to improve access to sign language services. Human rights legislation and other protections can also help ensure better inclusion for people with hearing loss.
