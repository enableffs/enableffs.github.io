Identification

Hearing impairment is considered to be hidden disability because it is not always obvious to know that a person has this disability. Persons who have a hearing impairment are, therefore, identified by observing their behavior and listening to the manner in which they express themselves and or interact with other people.

Some of the characteristics that may indicate that a person might have a hearing loss are the following:

  • Not responding to verbal instructions
  • Not responding to sounds
  • Not showing interest in verbal conversations
  • Frequent complaints of ear ache
  • Having a discharge in the ear
  • Giving inappropriate responses to verbal instructions
  • Inability to speak
  • Often staring at the speaker’s face
  • Missing out speech sounds in words

Persons who experience the above difficulties should be advised to undergo further assessment to establish their hearing status. The assessment may be a screening test or pure tone audiometry.

Screening

The above characteristics are not sufficient to enable one conclude that there is a hearing impairment. It is important to carry out a screening assessment in order to find out whether a person has a hearing problem or not.
The most widely used screening procedures for hearing loss involve measuring a person’s reaction to sounds. The following are some of the procedures used:

  1. Distraction test (suitable for children 6-18 months)
    In this test, a child is assessed for low and high frequency sounds. The child is seated on the parent/ guardian’s laps facing forward. The assessor then makes low and high frequency sounds one after another from behind, at a distance of one meter and at an angle of 45 degrees from each ear.

    The assessor should observe whether the child will react to the low frequency sounds and high frequency sounds. Failure to react to the sounds is an indication that the child may have a hearing loss.
  2. Cooperative test (suitable for children 18-30 months)
    This test requires the child to carry out actions with toys or other play materials following a verbal instruction from the assessor.

    The child is presented with the toy to play with. From behind the child, the assessor will give instructions to the child such as ’give it to mammy’ or, put it in the box’ etc. The instructions should be made at a distance of one meter from the child, and at an angle of 45 degrees from each ear.

    The assessor should observe whether the child will respond to the verbal instructions or reacts to his/her voice. Inability to carry out the instruction or react to it is an indication that the child may have a hearing loss.
  3. Performance test (suitable for children 2 ½ years and above)
    In this test, the child is expected to perform an activity following a ‘go’ command from the assessor. The child sits facing forward and is presented with some play materials. He /she is instructed to perform the activity (such as put the toy in a box) on hearing the ‘go ‘command.

The assessor has to observe whether the child is able to carry out the activity each time the command is given. Failure to carry out the activity when the commands are given is an indication that the child may have a hearing loss.

Pure tone Audiometry Test

Even if the person passes the screening tests, hearing loss can develop later. An audiometric assessment may be recommended for all persons who are suspected to have a hearing loss. This test also helps to find out more detail about the nature of the hearing loss (whether it is mild, moderate or severe) and the level in which each ear may be affected.

Conditions for developing communication skills

Deaf and hearing-impaired children often have a poor conceptual framework and difficulty to reflect on their own and others' feelings and experiences. The result can be can social difficulties and difficulties in making friends. Because of lack of basic concepts many children are struggling academically at school. For getting a good language development sign language environment is absolutely essential for these children. It is a precondition for social adjustment and for good mental health later in life to create an inner voice for thinking and understanding. It is essential that parents and children get the opportunity to communicate well right from the start. That's when the connection between parents and children formed and the interplay between them should start.
For hard of hearing children early communication with sign language in addition to speech is an advantage for the development of spoken language and concepts and have good impact on later reading and writing skills.

Audiogram

The audiogram is a graph which gives a detailed description of your hearing ability. It describes which sounds can be heard and which sounds are missing. The results of the measured hearing are characterized in a curve in the audiogram. The curve gives a visual representation of the hearing loss. An audiogram can give a picture of an individuals hearing, but it says nothing about how an individual utilizes auditory impressions. Two people with similar audiograms can have two very different experiences of hearing.

The "speech banana"

The "speech banana" in the audigram shows the area where the speaking voice is at normal hearing. Within this area are all the sounds of the letters themselves.

High frequency loss

High frequency loss (ski-slope loss) is the most common type of hearing loss. The line in the audiogram starts high and then falls off as the frequency increases. The result is that people with high frequency loss will have trouble hearing the sounds that distinguish consonants, and that will make it sound like people are mumbling. In this example the consonants "f", "g", "k", "s", "t" and th" become difficult to hear. In noisy surroundings it becomes difficult to understand speech. It is easier to hear male than female voices. Some ambient sounds, such as birds singing, are gone.

Cookiebite loss

A hearing loss that looks like a bite has been taken out of the middle of your audiogram is called a cookie bite loss and it is relatively rare. It means that you hear better in the low and high frequencies but less well in the middle frequencies. In this example many consonants, "ch", "g", "h", "k", "p", "r", "sh", and the vowel "a" are lost. Losses in this area affects:

  • speech intelligibility (mids and tweeters)
  • the perception of environmental sounds
  • make it difficult to understand speech in noisy surroundings.

Flat loss

A hearing loss which is decreased in all frequency ranges is called flat loss. Even if such a loss is relatively uncommon it can affect (depending on the size of the loss):

  • the speech intelligibility
  • the perception of environmental sounds
  • understanding speech in noisy surroundings
