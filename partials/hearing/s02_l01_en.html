Sound

Sound is described as waves or oscillation. Long waves gives the sound a bass character and short waves gives the sound treble characteristiscs.

The pitch of the waves are measured in Hertz (Hz) which is the number of cycles of waves per second.

  • A tone with many cycles per second we will experience as high, the tone has a high frequency.
  • A tone with fewer cycles per second we will experience as low, the tone has a low frequency.

The intensity of sound or the volume is measured in decibel (dB). Normal voice from the distance of 1 metre is around 60 dB and high music is around 100-110 dB. If the sound is around 110-130 dB most people will find it embarrassing.

Usually the sound moves around the air with the speed of 330 metres per second, but under water the sound moves much faster, around 1400 m/s.

Grades of hearing impairment

Hearing can vary from no impairment to deafness. The World Health Organisation (WHO) describe grades of hearing impairment as follow:

  • 0 - No impairment: 25 dB or better (better ear). No or very slight hearing problems. Able to hear whispers.
  • 1 - Slight impairment: 26-40 dB (better ear). Able to hear and repeat words spoken in normal voice at 1 metre.
    • Recommendations: Counselling. Hearing aids may be needed.
  • 2 - Moderate impairment: 41-60 dB (better ear). Able to hear and repeat words spoken in raised voice at 1 metre.
    • Recommendations: Hearing aids usually recommended.
  • 3 - Severe impairment: 61-80 dB (better ear). Able to hear some words when shouted into better ear.
    • Recommendations: Hearing aids needed. If no hearing aids available, lip-reading and signing should be taught.
  • 4 - Profound impairment including deafness: Unable to hear and understand even a shouted voice.
    • Recommendations: Hearing aids may help understanding words. Additional rehabilitation needed. Lip-reading and sometimes signing essential.

Grades 2, 3 and 4 are classified as disabling hearing impairment.

(Source: WHO)
