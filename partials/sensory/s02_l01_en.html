Inclusion

Inclusion is the practice of educating where the learners with impairment are in the same school and class alongside their non-disabled peers. The learners with impairment participate full as equally valued and unique individuals.
The Salamanca Statement and Framework for Action on Special Needs Education (1994) is arguably the most significant international document that has ever appeared in special education.
It argues that:

Regular schools with (an) inclusive orientation are the most effective means of combating discriminatory attitudes, creating welcoming communities, building an inclusive society and achieving education for all.

Unesco´s Guidelines for Inclusion (2005) states:

Education must be viewed as a facilitator in everyone’s human development and functionality, regardless of barriers of any kind, physical or otherwise. Therefore, disability of any kind cannot be a disqualifier.

According to this guidelines four key elements have tended to conceptualise inclusion:

  • Inclusion is a process.
    Inclusion has to be seen as a never-ending search to find better ways of responding to diversity.
  • Inclusion is concerned with the identification and removal of barriers.
    Collecting and evaluating information from a wide variety of sources in order to plan for improvements in policy and practice.
  • Inclusion is about the presence, participation and achievement of all students.
    Where children are educated and how punctually they attend, the quality of their experiences whilst they are there and the outcomes of learning across the curriculum.
  • Inclusion involves a particular emphasis on those groups of learners who may be at risk of marginalization, exclusion or underachievement.
    The moral responsibility to ensure that those groups are carefully monitored and that the necessary steps are taken.

What inclusion is about:

  • welcoming diversity
  • benefiting all learners, not only targeting the excluded
  • children in school who may feel excluded
  • providing equal access to education or making certain provisions for certain categories of children without excluding them

Segregation

Segregation is when the learners are in a special school where all the other learners are impaired in a segregated education setting. Such a school tend the learners to lead a separate life from the life outside school.

What inclusion is not about:

  • reforms of special education alone, but reform of both the formal and non-formal education system
  • responding only to diversity, but also improving the quality of education for all learners
  • special schools but perhaps additional support to students within the regular school system
  • meeting the needs of children with disabilities only
  • meeting one child’s needs at the expense of another child

Will the learners with impairment participate full and be valued equally and seen as unique individuals if they are integrated?

The process of inclusion




Integration is when the learner is being placed in mainstream education settings with some adaptations and resources. The structures are pre-existing and the attitudes are not changed. To be excluded is to be denied. In a segregated school the learner is accepted. Integration is based on understanding the learner. Inclusion is based on knowledge about the learner.
